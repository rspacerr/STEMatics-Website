Mental Health Awareness in Schools

by Leo Barrientos


What is Mental Health?

Mental health is defined as how one’s actions, thoughts, and feelings affect their mental wellbeing. Mental health is not set in stone, but it is ever-present and ever-changing depending on one’s circumstances. A mental illness is a condition that impacts someone’s thinking, emotions, and mood such that it interferes with their ability to function in settings like at home and school. About one in five kids living in the U.S. shows signs or symptoms of a mental illness in a given year.


Why are so many students and young people suffering from mental health problems?

Anxiety and depression are one of the largest problems facing Generation Z. issues, such as excessive alcohol or drug use, bullying, or teen pregnancy, which can exacerbate mental health difficulties. Within the last decade, rates of mental illness in youth have been on the rise.


Young people face pressures, such as achieving high marks and getting admitted into a prestigious college. In a study by Pew Research Center, 61% of teens from the ages of 13 to 17 reported feeling pressure to achieve good grades, which took a toll on their mental health. Michael H. Romanowski, Professor of Education at Qatar University, commented on this trend, “Unrestricted access to grades has developed into something unproductive and unhealthy, such as parents hovering over students. The process has reinforced the idea that grades are all that matters.” Other teens in the study cited pressure around social connections and others’ perception of them, such as judgement about how they dress or fitting in social circles at school and outside of school. Students also felt pressure to participate in extracurricular activities and to consistently perform well in sports. As shown in the study, young people are experiencing stressors surrounding school, peers, and their future. 59% of the teens surveyed planned to attend a 4-year college after they graduate high school, so there is overlap between students planning to attend a 4-year college and those who reported stress around grades in school. . This underscores the impact of pressure around school and one’s academic future on mental health.

The brain has been hardwired to immediately react to a perceived danger or threat, which goes back to prehistoric times when humans focused on survival and basic needs with no option of thinking towards the future. . Although humanity has evolved and we do not have to worry about day-to-day survival, our brains are still wired to think the way our ancestors did. A stressor revolving around the future, say for example, about college, is registered by the brain as a threat to our existence in the current moment. As Associate Professor of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences Mazen Kheirbek from the University of California states, “Anxiety is an emotional response to a perceived threat that’s not actually there in front of you”. Although such stresses do not affect the near future, our brain experiences a state of constant alarm and threat, which has long-term harms..


How to detect mental distress in students

One of the most identifiable signs that someone is experiencing mental or emotional distress is that they become more withdrawn and distant. This might manifest in an increase in absences from school and socially isolating from peers. The individual may have difficulty maintaining current friendships or fostering new ones. They may also experience difficulty maintaining attention and problem solving and may complete tasks slower. Over time, if not addressed, this may lead to a decline in academic performance, which creates a vicious cycle of stress over academics and frustration at results. Another hallmark of mental distress is a decrease in energy and interest in hobbies they usually enjoy, detracting time usually spent enjoying themself.


It is no wonder that only one third of students who have a mental illness go on to pursue post-secondary education. The earlier mental health concerns are detected and treated, the more likely it is that mental illness will not worsen and cause long-term damage. Early detection and intervention may lead to improved academic performance and allow young people to build necessary coping skills. .


How to help someone with a mental illness

To address academic-related stress and mental health issues, we need to identify and address these problems within the school community through initiatives such as Pass/Fail grades, more vacations and breaks, etc.. It is important to notice when a friend is becoming increasingly distant or withdrawn, support them, and point them towards resources and people who can help. Parents should also try to put less pressure on their children about grades and turn them into a positive challenge.
